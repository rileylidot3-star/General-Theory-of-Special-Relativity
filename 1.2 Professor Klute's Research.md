## 2. The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)
What is the LHC?

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is the world’s highest-energy particle accelerator. It collides protons (and sometimes lead ions) at extremely high energies to study the fundamental building blocks of matter.

Typical collision energy: 13 TeV (tera–electronvolts)

Collision rate: ~40 million collisions per second during operation

Why high energy? Higher energy collisions probe smaller distance scales and can create heavier, previously unobserved particles.

## Detectors at the LHC

The LHC contains several detectors, each designed for different research goals:

CMS (Compact Muon Solenoid): general-purpose detector studying a wide range of particles and phenomena

ATLAS: another general-purpose detector similar to CMS

LHCb: specialized in studying matter–antimatter asymmetry (why there is more matter than antimatter in the universe)

ALICE: focused on heavy-ion collisions and quark–gluon plasma, recreating conditions of the early universe

## Operational History

Began operating in 2009

Has undergone periodic shutdowns for upgrades to increase energy and intensity

Goal: explore higher center-of-mass energies to study deeper aspects of particle physics

## 3. Why High-Energy Collisions Matter
### Probing the Structure of Matter

High-energy collisions act like a microscope: higher collision energy → smaller structures can be resolved.

Enables detailed study of the proton’s internal structure, including quarks and gluons.

### Creating New Particles

According to E = mc², energy can be converted into mass, allowing production of new particles.

Collisions with sufficient energy can create massive or exotic particles

Main goal: discover unexpected or unknown particles

### Recreating the Early Universe

High-energy collisions produce hot, dense matter, similar to conditions fractions of a second after the Big Bang.

Allows study of exotic states of matter such as quark–gluon plasma

## 4. Mass and the Structure of Matter
### Hierarchy of Structure

Matter is organized in layers:
Everyday objects → molecules → atoms → nuclei → protons and neutrons → quarks and gluons

### Proton Structure

A proton is not elementary; it is made of:

Two up quarks

One down quark

Gluons (force carriers holding quarks together)

Transient quark–antiquark pairs popping in and out of existence

### Where Does Proton Mass Come From?

Proton mass: ~938 MeV (~1 GeV)

Quark masses contribute only a small fraction

Majority comes from energy stored in the gluon field, via E = mc²

### Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD)

QCD is the theory describing strong interactions among quarks and gluons.

Explains how gluon field energy gives rise to mass

Allows calculation of masses for composite particles called hadrons (protons, neutrons, etc.)

### 5. Elementary Particles
Composite vs Elementary

Protons are composite particles

Elementary particles have no known substructure, including:

Quarks

Leptons: electron, muon, tau

Neutrinos

### Particle Categories

Fermions (matter particles): quarks and leptons

Bosons (force carriers):

Photon → electromagnetic force

Gluons → strong force

W and Z bosons → weak force

### The Mystery

Some bosons (W, Z) are massive; others (photon, gluon) are massless

Fundamental question: why do particles have such widely different masses?

## 6. The Higgs Boson and the Origin of Mass
### Discovery

Higgs boson discovered in ~2012, confirming the Higgs mechanism

### Higgs Mechanism (Intuitive Picture)

The Higgs field permeates all space

Elementary particles interact with this field

Stronger interaction → higher inertia → larger mass

### Examples

Top quark: strong coupling → very heavy

Electron: weak coupling → light

Mass is determined by strength of interaction with Higgs field

## 7. Remaining Mysteries of Mass
### Open Problems

Particle masses span many orders of magnitude

Neutrino masses: extremely small, mechanism uncertain

Dark matter: inferred from galaxy behavior, not in Standard Model

Matter–antimatter asymmetry: universe dominated by matter

Gravity: not explained by Standard Model

Higgs mass problem: mass appears unnaturally small

## 8. How the CMS Detector Works
### Detector as a Camera

The CMS detector functions like a giant camera, recording the debris from proton collisions.

### Main Components

Silicon trackers: trace charged particle paths

Magnetic field (3.8 Tesla superconducting solenoid): curves charged particles → measures momentum

Electromagnetic calorimeter: measures energy of electrons and photons

Hadronic calorimeter: measures energy of hadrons (heavier particles)

Muon detectors: capture particles escaping inner layers

Proton beam pipe: where collisions occur

## 9. Higgs Candidate Events
### Example Decay Channels

Higgs → ZZ → 2 electrons + 2 muons

Higgs → 2 photons

### Reconstruction

Measure energies and trajectories of final particles

Reconstruct properties of the original particle: mass, coupling strength, decay modes

## 10. Experimental Results
### Higgs Coupling vs Mass

Observed linear relationship (log–log scale) between particle mass and Higgs coupling

Confirms: muons, taus, quarks acquire mass via Higgs mechanism
